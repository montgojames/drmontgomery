title: "Passing Out (Syncope)"

Passing out (syncope) is an *incredibly* common cause for an ER visit or hospital admission. There are many causes. Often the diagnosis is a retrospective best guess based on the totality of circumstantial evidence. Fundamentally, someone passes out when the whole brain suddenly loses blood flow (as opposed to a stroke, where only part of the brain loses blood flow, not the entire brain).

Syncope generally falls into a few large categories:

1. **Orthostatic** – This happens when someone stands up. Blood pressure from the veins to the heart is suddenly reduced by gravity. Everyone has experienced this — ever stood up too quickly and felt dizzy or lightheaded? That's orthostasis. The body compensates for this (with a faster heartbeat and blood vessel constriction), which is why after a few seconds you feel better; had it not compensated, you would have passed out. Anything which reduces the pressure in the venous system, or impairs the heart's compensation, contributes. Common culprits:
   - **Dehydration/volume depletion** – the "drier" you are, the more likely this is to happen. Reduced eating/drinking, sweating, taking fluid pills (diuretics), infection, etc.
   - **Medications** – most medications which treat high blood pressure (hypertension) or the heart can cause orthostasis. Certain prostate medications (tamsulosin/Flomax and others in the same group) are notorious for it. Certain psychiatric medications can also cause it. When evaluating someone for orthostasis, medications are my first stop — it's very common for medications taken for different problems to have additive side effects, leading to a large drop in blood pressure.
   - **Primary medical conditions** – many medical conditions cause orthostasis as part of the disease process. Parkinson's disease is a great example. Neuropathy (usually diabetic) also does.
   - It is very common for multiple reasons to come together to cause syncope. For example: an elderly man with Parkinson's and an enlarged prostate, taking Flomax and a fluid pill for his blood pressure, who didn't eat well for a day, stands up from bed and passes out.
   - **Urinating or having a bowel movement** – in order for us to release urine or stool, the body has to send a specific signal to allow the relevant muscles to relax. This same signal causes blood pressure to drop somewhat. Passing out from urinating is called micturition syncope. Common scenario: someone goes to the bathroom, urinates, stands up, and passes out. Waiting 15-30 seconds after finishing before standing up to wipe can avoid this.
2. **Vasovagal** – This type of syncope is caused by the vagus nerve. This nerve is more active during "rest and digest," and thus it slows heart rate and blood pressure. Generally, an unpleasant stimulus causes a sudden "fight or flight" impulse to start, and the vagus nerve activates to counteract it. In vasovagal syncope, the countering impulse overrides and abruptly reduces blood pressure and slows the heart rate, causing blood flow to the brain to suddenly drop, and one passes out. If someone hears bad news in a movie and passes out, this is why. Sudden pain, and especially **nausea**, are common causes. Most people feel a sequence of warmth, flushing, nausea/queasiness, sweating, tunnel vision, and then passing out. Some people have specific phobias that trigger it (like seeing blood). Humans are wired this way, and there isn't something "wrong" — it's the impulse that precipitated it.
3. **Cardiogenic** – This type of syncope comes from the heart. Usually an arrhythmia that suddenly changes the heart's electrical signal to either too fast or too slow. This sudden change causes blood pressure to plummet. Cardiogenic syncope is the most concerning type and can be a harbinger of future events. In the hospital, we monitor the heart's electrical signal (telemetry) for any arrhythmias which cause syncope. An ultrasound video of the heart (echocardiogram) is also frequently performed. This gives information on how the heart beats, chamber size, any valve problems, and other information. The weaker or more diseased a heart is, the higher the risk for arrhythmias.
4. **Seizure** – while not an actual cause of syncope, when someone loses consciousness we must determine whether it was because of a seizure or syncope. Some people twitch when they pass out, leading to an erroneous diagnosis of seizure, and not everyone who has a seizure convulses.
5. **Shock (distributive)** – Shock is a severe drop in blood pressure. Infections (septic shock), pulmonary embolism, pericardial tamponade, hemothorax/pneumothorax (blood or air improperly in the lung cavity), and other conditions can cause it. These generally cause persistent low blood pressure — when one passes out, it doesn't resolve in a few minutes.

As you can see, the differential diagnosis for syncope is vast and not always straightforward. Like patients, every case is different.

A common approach to treatment is the administration of intravenous fluids to hydrate, holding or adjusting offending medications, having the patient avoid standing up too quickly, watching how they respond, and conducting other tests to look for causes while giving the body time to correct itself.
