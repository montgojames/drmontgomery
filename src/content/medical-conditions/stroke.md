Most (85%) of strokes are caused by a blocked blood vessel; the other 15% are caused by a bleeding blood vessel. This article will focus on the more common blocked type.

Strokes are caused by a blood vessel in the brain becoming blocked. Whatever brain tissue was behind that blockage starts to become injured. If blood flow is returned quickly, the injured tissue can recover; like all injured things, if it doesn't get better, it dies — this dead tissue is the stroke. If blood flow returns spontaneously quickly enough that the brain tissue does not die, we call that a TIA (transient ischemic attack), or more commonly, a "mini-stroke." All strokes, in essence, start as a TIA; if blood flow returns and the patient's symptoms resolve to normal with no damage seen on brain imaging (like a head CT scan or brain MRI), we'll call it a TIA. If we see damage on the imaging, we call it a stroke. They are both treated the same way.

Where the stroke occurs determines what symptoms the patient has. If it's in the language center, the patient may not be able to speak; if it's in the balance center, they may be unsteady; if it's in the section that controls movement, they may not be able to move a body part. For the most part, each side of the brain controls half the body, so strokes tend to affect only one side.

Brain cells begin to die at the start of a stroke; for this reason, the faster someone can be treated, the more brain tissue is saved and the better they'll do. A stroke is a "brain attack" with the same urgency as a "heart attack."

When patients come to the ER soon (currently within 3 hours) after the onset of stroke symptoms, doctors can decide whether or not to give a powerful blood thinner (think of it as a blood clot Drain-O) to break up the clot and return blood flow to the affected brain. The potency of this blood thinner means there are many reasons it *cannot* be safely given. Every patient must be individually evaluated, and a judgment call made by the doctors as to whether the risk of harm is less than the benefit of improving the stroke symptoms.

After this immediate window, strokes are treated with a combination of blood thinners, cholesterol medications, and, most importantly, physical and occupational therapy. Even when a stroke damages the brain, it has some ability to heal — this is how people recover from a stroke.
