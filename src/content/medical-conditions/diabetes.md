Diabetes Mellitus is when the body cannot properly produce enough insulin, and thus cannot properly regulate blood sugars. Abnormal blood sugars are the consequence, not the cause.

There are 2 main types of diabetes: Type 1 and Type 2.

**Type 1:** You don't **make** insulin. The body's immune system has attacked and destroyed the cells in the pancreas which made insulin. This autoimmune disease is diagnosed mostly in children or adolescents. It **requires** insulin to manage. No insulin = death.

**Type 2:** Your body makes insulin, but over many years the body becomes resistant to that insulin. The body makes more, which it in turn becomes resistant to, and the cycle continues. This is what adults develop, particularly when overweight.

Think of insulin like money, and blood sugar as groceries. Now add inflation: insulin buys groceries for a while, but as the groceries cost more, you need more insulin to buy the same thing. This cycle continues until you start running out of money (insulin); now you can't control your groceries (and blood sugars rise). Exercise causes your body to use blood sugar in a way that bypasses the need for insulin — in the scenario above, like getting free groceries. This is why exercise is so highly recommended for diabetics; getting something for free is far better than continuing to pay more.

In the hospital we primarily use insulin. This gives us more control over sugars and avoids problems oral medications can cause, like: interactions with other medications, interactions with the dye used in CT scans, or being on a medication but then not being allowed to eat before a procedure. We hold home diabetes pills and use insulin while in the hospital; at discharge, we can either resume or change the pills, or prescribe new insulin if needed.

The diabetic educator at Chippenham is a great resource, especially for new diabetics. If you want to learn more about diabetes or its management, request a consult from the diabetic educator.

The main issue with diabetes is that excess sugar over time damages tissues, particularly blood vessels and nerves — starting small and getting larger.

**Nerve damage:** diabetic neuropathy. Numbness, tingling, burning, a "funny bone" sensation. Often begins in the toes of both feet and works its way up the leg like a pair of socks. Can happen in the fingers too. Many other nerves can be damaged as well.

**Blood vessels:** diabetes causes damage to blood vessels, which narrows and eventually blocks them. If the affected blood vessels are in the legs, you'll have poor circulation (peripheral arterial disease); in the heart's arteries, it causes heart attacks (coronary artery disease); in the brain, it causes strokes (cerebrovascular accidents), particularly a type called lacunar strokes, which are like trimming the tips of branches from the brain's blood vessel "tree."

A blood test called A1c gives us a 3-month average of blood sugar levels. Even if a reading is high or low today, A1c provides insight into how the patient's sugars have been controlled and informs the treatment plan. Non-diabetics are below 6. A range of 6-10 can often be treated with oral medications. Once A1c is above 10, most people need insulin.

Low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) occurs when sugars are below 70. People can feel confused, sweaty, jittery/shaky, and generally unwell. Eating something sweet will bring the sugar level back up.
