Atrial fibrillation and atrial flutter are very common arrhythmias. Tens of millions of people are affected — 13% of the population over 65 years old. While not the same, they are closely related, like "brothers or cousins" of one another. Both arrhythmias come from abnormal electrical discharges in the top heart chambers (atria). They fire very fast (up to 300 beats per minute), and though slowed through the heart's electrical relay (AV node), they still make the heart beat faster than normal.

**Basic heart electricity:** In the top right heart chamber (atrium), the heart's main electrical pacemaker (sinus node) sends electricity down special conductive pathways until it reaches the relay station (AV node). Here the signal is sent down more special pathways into the lower heart chambers (ventricles), where it branches to make both the right and left sides beat together.

As a baseball analogy: the pitcher throws to the catcher at a steady rhythm (normal sinus rhythm). Now, if the entire infield also started throwing to the catcher, he'd experience many baseballs coming fast and irregular and have difficulty keeping up (atrial fibrillation). Atrial flutter is similar but more rhythmic and less irregular.

We fix atrial fibrillation the same way you'd fix this analogy — either stop the infield from throwing baseballs, or at least slow them down so the catcher can keep up.

**Atrial Fibrillation (A. fib):**

Electrical current makes the heart pump, so if you have an electrical problem, you may also have a pump problem. Classically, atrial fibrillation causes the heart to beat fast (between 100-200 bpm) and irregularly. When it's too fast, the heart can't refill with blood in time, so less blood is pumped out. Even though it's going faster, less blood is pumped from the heart to the brain, causing dizziness or passing out (syncope).

The second problem is that this irregular electrical current makes the top chambers of the heart quiver or tremor instead of pumping rhythmically. Blood can pool in certain areas of the heart (the left atrial appendage) and form a blood clot. If this clot travels to the lower heart chamber, it can be pumped to the brain, causing a stroke (about 1 in 4 strokes come from A. fib).

Medical treatment of A. fib addresses both the electrical/pump issue and stroke risk.

**A. fib treatment:**

1. **Electrical:**
   - Get the rhythm back to normal.
   - Give medications which can help the heart switch from A. fib back to a normal rhythm.
   - Temporarily reset the heart through an electrical shock (direct current cardioversion).
   - Perform a procedure inside the heart (ablation) which "permanently" fixes atrial fibrillation (ablations have about an 85% success rate).
   - Use a pacemaker to take over the electrical control of the heart.
2. **Reduce stroke risk:**
   - Give blood thinners, which reduce the risk that blood pooling in the heart will form a clot and cause a stroke.
   - The use of blood thinners is very individualized to the patient — their age, other health problems, risk of bleeding, and the chance treatment will reduce stroke risk are all considered.
   - There are calculators available to help weigh the risks versus benefits. Overall, blood thinners reduce the *relative* risk of stroke by 50-66%. Example: if you had a 9% risk of stroke, blood thinners reduce it to 3-5% — NOT from a 100% chance down to 33%.

     - [CHA₂DS₂-VASc Calculator](https://chadsva.org/)
     - [HAS-BLED Calculator](https://www.mdcalc.com/calc/807/has-bled-score-major-bleeding-risk)
   - Common blood thinners:
     - Pills: warfarin (Coumadin), apixaban (Eliquis), rivaroxaban (Xarelto), dabigatran (Pradaxa)
     - IV or injectable: enoxaparin (Lovenox), heparin
     - Non-medicine: Watchman is a device implanted in the top chamber of the heart (left atrial appendage) which reduces the risk of stroke from A. fib without blood thinners; reserved for those whose bleeding risk is too great with medication.

**Atrial Flutter** – very similar to A. fib. The pathway of the electrical signal is different. It carries a risk of stroke and is treated similarly to A. fib, and thus, from a patient's perspective, can be considered a different side of the same coin.

**Note:** There are other blood thinners, like clopidogrel (Plavix) or ticagrelor (Brilinta), which are for the heart or strokes, but these DO NOT treat A. fib or A. flutter. Do not presume your blood thinner will reduce stroke risk in A. fib without talking with your doctor.
