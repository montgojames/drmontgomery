**"Discharge planning starts at admission."**

This is a common adage we ascribe to in hospitalist medicine. Waiting until the person is "ready to go" to start planning for their exit from the hospital is a road paved in delays, problems, and broken expectations. The time after a patient discharges from the hospital until they again see an outpatient provider is a high-risk time. New diagnoses, medications, testing results, and specialist involvement, along with follow-up appointments, all come together to befuddle the patient right as they are set out on their own. I hope this section helps to demystify the process and provide you with direction to ensure things don't fall through the cracks after discharge.

## Discharge location: "Where are you going?"

1. **Home** – either alone or with family. Easiest and most straightforward solution. Checklist:
   1. Am I well/strong enough to be at home?
      - If not, who is going to take care of me?
      - What about when they are working? Out of town?
      - Do I have access to groceries, medications, communication (phone, internet)?
   2. Do I have all needed equipment? (i.e., walker, bedside commode, shower chair, hospital bed, wheelchair, toilet riser, grab bars for bathroom, Hoyer lift, etc.)
   3. Do I have a **RIDE HOME** and a **KEY TO GET IN?**
      - Friend or family picking you up? Are they available? What time are they coming?
      - Commercial (medical transportation company, taxi, Uber/Lyft)?
        - Insurance often **DOES NOT** pay for transportation home (there are exceptions).
   4. Do you have clothes to wear?
2. **Home with Home Health** – Same as above, except now a physical therapist, occupational therapist, and nurse will come visit. Generally 2-3 times a week for 30-60 minute visits.
   - These are NOT personal care aides who provide custodial care (bathing, dressing, etc.).
   - Personal care aides are private pay out of pocket; insurance does not cover this (except some long-term care policies and Medicaid).
3. **Skilled Nursing Facilities (SNF)** – there are many around. Each has long-term care (traditional "nursing home") and acute care facilities. Medicare and most insurances will pay for 21 days in a SNF after discharge.
   - VA State Medicaid is the only form of insurance which pays for long-term care (living in a nursing home).
   - The patient has to be both willing to go and willing and able to participate in therapy.
     - If unable to participate due to mental abilities (i.e., dementia, confusion, etc.) or physical limitations (contractures, cannot work with hospital therapist), then insurance won't pay.
     - The patient must agree to go. Many times I have seen a patient's children try to force their parent into a SNF. Often they invoke power of attorney to try to force their parents. Power of Attorney is only applicable when the patient *is unable* to make a decision — *not when they make a decision you do not like.* Mentally capable adults are allowed to make medical decisions we disagree with.
   - Have a plan for where you will go (home) once therapy is completed in 3 weeks.
   - Expect to do about an hour of therapy most days of the week. This is highly variable based on the patient's individual need and facility staffing.
   - You live there while doing therapy. You are able to leave to attend doctors' appointments or go out with your family as you desire.
   - Staffing is less than in a hospital. If you require *more* care than you did in a hospital, consider privately hiring a private duty care aide to supplement the care.
   - Medicare and other insurances require that the patient be provided a choice of facilities (Medicare Choice Letter).
     - [US News and World Report Nursing Home Rankings for Richmond](https://health.usnews.com/best-nursing-homes/area/richmond-va)
     - [Medicare Comparison Website](https://www.medicare.gov/care-compare/results?searchType=NursingHome&page=1&city=Richmond&state=VA&zipcode=&radius=25&providerRating_overall=2,1&sort=closest)
     - Website rankings are *highly* variable, and, like most online rankings, unhappy people tend to leave more reviews than happy people.
     - If possible, have a family member tour the facility to get a feel for it.
     - Often location relative to one's home and facility bed availability influence choice.
4. **Inpatient Rehab (IPR)** – the highest level of post-hospital rehab. Medicare requires the patient tolerate *3 hours a day* of therapy **and** needs physician onsite care.
   - Around Richmond, IPRs include:
     - [Johnston-Willis](https://www.hcavirginia.com/locations/johnston-willis-hospital/specialties/physical-therapy-and-rehabilitation) – 5th floor of Johnston-Willis Hospital.
     - [Sheltering Arms](https://www.shelteringarms.com/) – Broad St in Short Pump.
     - [Encompass](https://encompasshealth.com/locations/richmondrehab) – has 2 locations in Richmond and Petersburg.
   - Process of getting into IPR:
     1. Physical and Occupational Therapy see and recommend IPR-level rehab.
     2. Doctor places an order for IPR (order is for case manager).
     3. Case manager obtains the patient's choice of IPR.
     4. Case manager submits the therapy notes and other clinical information to the IPR for review.
     5. Staff at the IPR reviews the notes and either accepts or declines.
     6. If the IPR accepts, that facility then submits a request to the insurance company for approval. **MOST IPR DENIALS HAPPEN HERE, FROM INSURANCE.** Some insurances (Humana, United Health) have an effective 100% denial rate.
     7. If insurance approves the IPR benefit, the patient is transferred there when a bed is available (can go by medical transport or private vehicle).
        - If insurance denies the IPR benefit, the patient generally goes to a SNF or goes home with home health.
     8. This usually takes 2-5 days to complete (most is waiting on insurance).
5. **Long-Term Acute Care (LTAC) Hospital** – a specific type of hospital made for patients who are stable but remain too ill to go home or to other rehabs, yet do not require ongoing acute hospitalization. Estimated lengths of stay are 3-4 weeks, instead of 3-4 days. Weaning from a ventilator, prolonged pulmonary or cardiac recovery, complex wound care, brain injury, and recovery after catastrophic illness are some examples.
   - [Select Specialty Hospital](https://www.selectspecialtyhospitals.com/locations-and-tours/va/richmond/richmond/) in Richmond is the local LTAC.
6. **Hospice** – more detailed information on hospice is found under the Hospice topic. In short, most hospice is done at home. Medicare covers hospice care at 100%, and thus sets the regulations.
   - **Home Hospice** – the patient goes home, and a hospice nurse and nurse's aide come out to make visits several times a week. Care is performed by the patient's family.
     - There are some Hospice Houses – private residences where several hospice patients live and staff care for them. There are few available.
   - **Hospice in a facility (SNF, assisted living)** – the patient lives at the facility, and hospice comes in and performs visits the same way they would at home.
     - Medicare DOES NOT cover room and board for hospice care at a SNF. They cover the medical care, but the patient must private pay for room and board (generally several thousand dollars a month).
   - **General Inpatient Hospice (GIP)** – this is for patients whose death is imminent (prognosis a few days; < 7) and whose symptoms cannot be managed outside of a hospital setting. They require IV medication for symptom control and constant nurse attention.
     - From CMS: "General inpatient care (GIP) is available to all hospice beneficiaries who are in need of pain control or symptom management that cannot be provided in any other setting. Skilled nursing care may be needed by a patient whose home support has broken down if this breakdown makes it no longer feasible to furnish needed care in the home setting. GIP is not intended to be custodial or residential. Once a beneficiary's symptoms are stabilized, or pain is managed, he/she must return to a routine level of care. The beneficiary may remain in a facility due to safety, but Medicare will not pay for GIP unless the beneficiary is in need of this level of care, and it is clearly documented in the medical records."
7. **Inpatient Psychiatric Facility** – hospitals are the safety net for an acute mental health crisis in the United States. Often patients are admitted to a medical hospital to stabilize the consequences of their mental health crisis (i.e., suicide attempt, acute substance intoxication or withdrawal, etc.). Once this stabilizes, and after assessment by a psychiatrist, they may be transferred to an inpatient psychiatric hospital (Tucker's Pavilion at Chippenham Hospital) for ongoing psychiatric stabilization.
   - To be admitted to a psychiatric hospital, the psychiatrist HAS TO RECOMMEND ADMISSION. The patient, their families, and the medical doctor cannot overrule this decision.
     - If the patient goes voluntarily, they generally stay 3-7 days and then discharge home.
     - If the patient DOES NOT WANT TO GO, they can be committed to the psychiatric hospital *involuntarily.* This is most common after suicide attempts, which effectively mandates an inpatient psychiatric stay when the patient doesn't want to go.
       - If the psychiatrist recommends an inpatient admission and the patient refuses, then the Richmond Behavioral Health Authority (RBHA) is involved. Their representative conducts an assessment (by phone or in person) and has the **final decision on admission or not** (overrules everyone else, including doctors).
       - If RBHA does NOT think admission is warranted, the patient is discharged home.
       - If RBHA feels admission IS warranted, they contact a magistrate (judge) who issues a Psychiatric Temporary Detainment Order (TDO). This is a *legal* order compelling someone to inpatient treatment against their will. Security/Police then escort the patient to Tucker's for care. DOCTORS CANNOT COMMIT PEOPLE, ONLY JUDGES.
         - Because this is a legal order, it becomes part of one's legal record and can be used in court. Also, many legal forms require attestation that one has never been involuntarily committed to a psychiatric hospital (for instance, concealed carry handgun permits).
   - Once it has been determined a patient is going to Tucker's, they must wait for a bed. At Chippenham Hospital and Tucker's this is often several days due to patient volumes.

## Discharge planner: Case Manager / Social Worker

Case Managers (Social Workers) are the underappreciated lynchpin of discharge planning. For any of the above discharge plans to work, they have to arrange it. Most hospital units have 1-2 social workers on that floor, and nearly everyone there needs something from the list above. Their job is to make these discharge arrangements, and to do so within the confines of Medicare, private insurance, no insurance, rehab facility, and patient and family demands.

Making the case manager's life easier facilitates and expedites the patient's care:

1. Think about what your discharge plan will be; if other than going home alone, start planning for what you want.
2. If you need a facility, start looking them up online. Have family members visit if able; come up with a list of 2-3 places ranked in order of preference.
3. Look up online or contact the insurance company to figure out what your after-hospital care coverage is. Did the hospital get your insurance card on admission?
4. How are you going to get there? Plan for a ride by calling family/friends to ensure they are available to pick you up. If you need medical transportation, let the case manager know that early.

## Discharge Process

You are medically ready. Discharge arrangements have been made. A ride is in place. How do you actually get out of the hospital?

1. **Attending physician places a discharge order.** Most patients are admitted under the hospitalist, making this physician the attending. It is not unusual for a consultant to tell a patient they are "ready to go," but it is still the hospitalist who has to discharge the patient. They ensure the medications and above discharge plans are completed before the patient leaves.
2. **Attending physician completes the medication reconciliation.** This combines all of your home meds, hospital meds, and any new meds into one list telling the patient exactly what to take when they get home.
   - *More often than not,* this list is **inaccurate** when I discharge a patient. Much effort is expended in correcting it. Medication errors and drug-drug interactions are a leading cause of patient harm, and this is a prime set-up for it.
   - **PRIOR TO DISCHARGE, REVIEW YOUR HOME MEDICATIONS WITH THE NURSE TO ENSURE ACCURACY OF THE MEDICATION RECONCILIATION.**
3. **Attending physician completes the discharge note,** which is the information for the patient.
4. **The nurse reviews** the discharge medication reconciliation and discharge note with the patient/family.
5. **The nurse removes the patient's IV** and, if needed, the cardiac monitor leads.
6. **The patient is taken in a wheelchair down to the discharge location.**
   - Discharge pick-up is by the cafeteria and Chick-fil-A.
   - Patients who need to wait several hours for a ride are taken to wait in the discharge lobby, which is in the main hospital lobby by Starbucks. This frees up the room to be cleaned so patients waiting in the ER can get up to the hospital floor.

## Home oxygen arrangement

It is common for patients to need oxygen after discharge when they go home. Medicare and other insurance companies have set the following criteria: within 48 hours of hospital discharge, the patient's room air oxygen saturation must be < 88% to qualify for home oxygen. Insurance won't pay for oxygen because you want it, feel better with it, or need it "just in case."

If patients are found to have low oxygen levels (hypoxia) when sleeping, home oxygen can be arranged prior to discharge; however, in order to get a CPAP/BiPAP/Trilogy machine for sleep apnea, a sleep study must be done as an outpatient.

## Who does what

Leaving the hospital is a multi-step process with several providers all playing different roles to ensure the patient is sent home safely and informed. The goal is to have the patient out of the hospital within 2 hours of the doctor discharging the patient.

**Doctor** – Makes the determination that the patient is medically ready for discharge. If multiple providers are involved in the patient's care, they come to a consensus on when to discharge. The doctor must enter the discharge order, addressing topics like diet, activity, follow-up, any restrictions, special instructions for going home, and things to look out for. Reviews all medications, including home medications and new medications, and sends new prescriptions to the patient's pharmacy (done electronically). Certain medications (controlled substances, opioids) have restrictions on who and how many can be prescribed at once. Composes the patient's discharge note, combining the above information into the packet given to the patient, and the discharge summary on the patient's hospital stay so other providers (PCP, future doctors) understand what happened. This is sent to the patient's PCP by the medical records department.

**Nurse** – Prepares the patient for discharge by giving any medication prior to leaving, removing peripheral IVs and cardiac monitor, and helping the patient get dressed and collect their belongings, coordinating with the patient's ride on when and where to pick them up. A specialized discharge nurse sometimes helps the primary nurse to expedite the process. Reviews the discharge packet and medications with the patient to ensure all questions are answered.

**Case Manager** – Secures any equipment (i.e., home oxygen, a walker) and services (i.e., home health) the patient needs.

## Tips for the patient to improve discharge

Once admitted to the hospital, develop a plan for going home:

1. Where are you going? (home, stay with family, rehab center)
2. How will you get there? (family drives, social worker arranges transportation)
3. Do you need anything? (bedside commode, walker, wheelchair, hospital bed)
4. Do you already have or need home services? (home health nursing, physical or occupational therapy)
5. Do you have enough medications at home? (refills needed, run out of meds)
6. Do you need a work/school note?
7. Do you have clothes to wear to go home? (if not, family should bring in)
8. Do you already have any scheduled doctor's appointments to follow up with after discharge?
