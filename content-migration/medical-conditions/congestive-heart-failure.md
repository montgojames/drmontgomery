**What is CHF?**

Congestive Heart Failure (CHF) is a clinical syndrome where the heart's blood-pumping efficiency is decreased. The term "failure" is really a misnomer — "Decreased Heart Pumping Efficiency" is a more accurate description. This decreased efficiency leads to fluid (the liquid part of blood) backing up, starting in the lungs, but people generally develop swelling in the ankles and legs as it progresses.

**What's wrong with the heart in CHF?**

The heart is a springy muscle. Blood enters, compresses the spring, and as it rebounds the muscle contracts, pumping blood from the heart. CHF can be thought of as a problem with the spring, and comes in 2 primary types. A normal heart beats out 60-65% of the blood it holds with each beat.

1. **Systolic / Heart Failure with Reduced Ejection Fraction (HFrEF)** – In this type of heart failure the heart muscle has grown weak. Once your ejection fraction is < 50%, we consider this HFrEF. Think of a spring which has been overstretched and lost its "springiness." With each heartbeat, less blood is pumped out; if less exits, more backs up.
2. **Diastolic / Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction (HFpEF)** – In this type of heart failure the heart still beats out 60-65%. However, the muscle has become stiff, like a rusted spring. A rusted spring doesn't compress or bounce back with normal "springiness." With each heartbeat, less blood is pumped out; if less exits, more backs up.
3. As a sports analogy: imagine catching a basketball pass. To catch it, your arms are partially extended and bent, so when you catch the ball, your arm muscles decelerate it toward your chest, absorbing the energy. Now play with an arm cast on — you can't absorb the energy correctly; this is HFpEF. Throwing the ball is like systolic (HFrEF) — it's just a weak pass; it may get the job done, but not well.
4. One can have both. Generally diastolic (HFpEF) comes first, but as the heart wears out over time from poor pumping efficiency, it becomes weak too — and thus systolic (HFrEF) develops as well; double trouble.

**What are the [statistics](https://onlinejcf.com/article/S1071-9164(24)00232-X/fulltext) on CHF?**

1. **Prognosis:** In general, the prognosis with CHF declines about 10% per year. So after 1 year, 90% of people with CHF are still alive; after 5 years, 50% of people are still alive. While this can be bleak, improvements in CHF treatment have made it possible for people to live long beyond this.
2. **Incidence:** About 7 million people in the US have CHF. Risk increases as you get older; lifetime risk is 25% (1 in 4 people). 425,000 people die of CHF each year, and CHF accounts for half of cardiovascular deaths. Somewhat ironically, death certificates in Virginia do not permit listing heart failure as a cause of death.
3. **Hospital Readmission:** CHF is the most common cause of hospitalization in the US for people older than 65. It has the highest 30-day re-hospitalization rate among medical and surgical conditions, accounting for up to [27%](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7186095/) of total readmission rates.

Congestive Heart Failure requires a multi-pronged approach. Diet, exercise, medications, doctor visits, home monitoring, and sometimes advanced invasive procedures are all part of a comprehensive treatment plan.

1. **Diet** – A *lower*-salt diet. **Not** no salt. I tell patients to spare the salt shaker and avoid highly salt-containing foods (soup, TV dinners, some Asian cuisine).
2. **Fluid intake** – If you have swelling (edema) from CHF, you need to watch how much fluid you take in. Too much just ends up in your skin. The general rule is 2000 mL (2 L or 64 oz) per day. All fluid counts: tea, water, soda, etc. If it's wet and you drink it, count it toward the total.
3. **Exercise** – Exercise is good for CHF; it can help it improve. A referral to Cardiac Rehab means gradual exercise increase under the watchful eye of a physical therapist. If you don't have cardiac rehab available, progressive walking is a good start. Every day, try to walk a little further.
4. **Medications** – This section could be a book unto itself. All medications come with benefits and side effects. I try to get people on the "best" combination; however, each patient has limiting factors (blood pressure, heart rate, kidney function, electrolytes, interactions with other medications, cost, etc.) which mean they cannot take them all. The "best" combination is the one the person can take with little to no side effects.
   - **Beta-blockers** – Common examples: metoprolol, carvedilol. These relax the heart muscle somewhat and reduce blood pressure and heart rate.
   - **ACE Inhibitors / ARB receptor blockers** – Common examples: losartan, lisinopril, valsartan. These affect molecules released by the kidney and affect blood pressure, kidney function, and electrolytes.
   - **Entresto** – This is an ARB receptor blocker combined with another medication. It is the only one in its class.
   - **Mineralocorticoid antagonists** – spironolactone and eplerenone. These block a hormone in the body which absorbs salt, and also affect how the heart reshapes itself over time.
   - **SGLT2 Inhibitors** – The new kids on the block. Made as a medication for diabetes, but studies show they can have beneficial effects on the heart. Very expensive because they're new.
   - **Diuretics ("fluid pills")** – All the backed-up fluid in the lungs or legs needs to come out. Medications in this class increase urination and remove fluid. The downside is they can dehydrate you and affect your electrolytes (primarily potassium and magnesium), so they need monitoring.
5. **Doctor's Visits** – Because CHF medications alter your blood pressure, heart rate, salt/water management, and kidney function, they are not set-and-forget. Like a tightrope walker, if things go too far one way, an adjustment is needed. Expect to have medications tweaked to optimize their effect. Consistent follow-up with doctors is imperative to keeping you healthy and out of the hospital with CHF.
6. **Home Monitoring** – Picking up on subtle changes in weight and extra edema early is key. This permits interventions that can steer the patient back on track before things get worse and lead to hospital admission. Weighing yourself most days of the week is a good start — you can't gain pounds of fat in a few days, so if your weight increases, it's all water weight. Tracking your blood pressure and pulse is also important. Many doctors' offices and home health agencies have special CHF programs. Be proactive and engaged in your health! "An ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure." — Benjamin Franklin, 1735
