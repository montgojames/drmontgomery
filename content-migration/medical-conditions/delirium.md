Confusion in the hospital is extremely common. Roughly [50%](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10299512/)<sup>1</sup> of hospitalized patients experience delirium, frequently increasing to 80% if in the ICU. About a third of delirious patients go unrecognized, primarily because they are hypoactive — meaning that, instead of being agitated and acting out, they are more withdrawn into themselves and thus easier to miss.

It must be stated up front that delirium is **NOT** the same thing as dementia. Delirium is a sudden change in thinking, generally over hours to days, that occurs as a result of another causative factor. Dementia is the steady, irreversible decline in brain function over months to years. I have seen numerous families told the patient "developed dementia" in the hospital, which is inaccurate.

Having seen thousands of delirious patients, recognition can be straightforward. Just like a family member knows something is wrong, just not sure what — generally, a confused, inattentive patient, unsure of time and circumstances, is delirious.

The standard validated way to diagnose delirium is known as the [Confusion Assessment Method (CAM)](https://geriatrictoolkit.missouri.edu/cog/Confusion-Assessment-Method-delirium.pdf): Acute onset and fluctuating course, AND inattention, AND *either* disorganized thinking or altered level of consciousness.

**How to identify delirium:**

1. **New (acute)** – it's a new change **and it fluctuates.** Not chronic thinking problems (i.e., dementia).
2. **Inattention** – generally the first sign, before disorientation develops. You have to redirect or get their attention mid-conversation, or the patient keeps missing parts of the conversation while listening to you.
3. **Variable** – it comes and goes, waxing and waning over hours to days. Can be as short as during a single conversation.
4. **Altered level of consciousness** – lethargic, hard to awaken, hyperactive, easily startled — not their normal level of wakefulness.
5. **Disorganized thinking pattern** – rambling thoughts, illogical statements, not connecting the dots, jumping back and forth between the current topic and something unrelated.
6. **Disoriented** – can't answer simple questions like: What is your name and birthdate? Where are you? What year is it? How did you get here?
7. **Memory impairment** – forgetting things mid-interview; asking the same questions over and over.
8. **Perception disturbances** – hallucinations; seeing things/people who aren't there; thinking there are bugs on the walls or crawling on their skin.
9. **Psychomotor alterations** –
   - Hyperactive: restless, fidgety, can't sit still, picking at clothes, IV tubing, whatever their hands find.
   - Hypoactive: sluggish, staring off into space, abnormally slow in speech, thought, or action.

**What causes delirium?** In short: the hospital. A great irony of healthcare is that people come to hospitals to get better, and a multitude of factors come together to cause harm.

There are many risk factors which contribute to delirium in the hospital. Examples include (in roughly descending order of risk):

1. **Medication side effects & drug-drug interactions (i.e., polypharmacy).** This is the biggest cause of iatrogenic (medical-care-caused) delirium. So many medications we prescribe cause confusion as a side effect. Every medication has to be weighed for its likelihood to help versus its risk of harm.
   - Common culprit medications:
     - Opioid pain medications: morphine, oxycodone, hydrocodone, Dilaudid (hydromorphone), fentanyl, and to a lesser extent tramadol
     - Benzodiazepines: Xanax (alprazolam), Valium (diazepam), Ativan (lorazepam), Versed (midazolam), Klonopin (clonazepam), Ambien (zolpidem), Librium, and others
     - Anticholinergics: these medications have many different roles but all work through a similar pathway — Benadryl, phenergan, compazine, meclizine, hydroxyzine, Bentyl
     - Neuropathy medications: Neurontin (gabapentin), Lyrica (pregabalin), amitriptyline, nortriptyline
     - Mental health medications: excluding most SSRIs (Prozac, Lexapro, Zoloft, etc.) and SNRIs (Effexor, Cymbalta), nearly everything prescribed for mental health treatment has the potential to cause confusion. They're a mixed bag — these can both greatly improve and greatly worsen delirium. This is where an experienced hospitalist really has to make subtle judgment calls regarding medications.
2. **Pre-existing cognitive impairment.** Dementia is the most prominent example. A patient with underlying dementia is very likely to become *more* confused in the hospital. One need not have "full" dementia — mild dementia, impaired cognitive function, or being easily confused/overwhelmed or forgetful all increase the risk. It is a spectrum; the more difficulty your brain has with day-to-day tasks, the higher the proportional chance it will not work well (i.e., become confused/delirious) when subjected to extra stress.
   - Mental health disorders — same underlying idea. Mild symptoms do not impair daily functioning, but if the brain has severe mental health disorders at baseline, there is a good possibility the hospital will exacerbate them.
3. **Dehydration.** This one can be easy to fall into and easy to overlook. Older people have many risk factors: often don't eat or drink as much, are told to avoid fluid intake due to a heart, liver, or kidney condition, are taking fluid pills (diuretics), don't have ready access to food and water if mobility is impaired, or have brain problems preventing them from eating/drinking (dementia, stroke, MS, etc.). To make things worse, as fluid intake goes down, the kidneys work less. This causes both a buildup of natural toxins *and* less removal of normal medications, so their levels build up and become toxic — even at a normal dose.
   - "Lights on but nobody's home" — this is how I best describe dehydration on an elderly mind. Often patients are awake but tend to blankly stare; they can respond to questions but are either slow to process, get lost while speaking, or don't respond at all. Quietly staring at a wall or looking around is common.
   - Electrolyte problems and the need for an indwelling urinary catheter (Foley) often go hand in hand with this.
4. **Disruption of the normal sleep cycle / circadian rhythm.** One of the largest, most underappreciated causes — and a primary SOLUTION to hospital-acquired delirium.
   - If you're reading this in a hospital, it's probable you haven't had good sleep lately. The effects of illness, medication side effects, noise, lab draws, staff interruptions, and the unrelenting beeping of your IV pump all add up.
   - At some point, most of us have had to perform at work or school on little to no sleep. Remember how sluggish, irritable, and scattered your concentration was? Now age your brain, make yourself sick, add in jet lag from a disrupted circadian rhythm, and sprinkle in some medication side effects. Delirium seems almost inevitable.
   - This is a personal pet peeve of mine in the hospital: not taking the need for recuperative sleep seriously, and then being surprised when confusion develops.

Delirium is bad for you. Pick any health metric — length of hospital stay, risk of falls, time to recover, medication needed for behavior, risk of death ([25-33% mortality rate](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002934399000704)), being discharged somewhere other than home (i.e., a skilled nursing facility), risk of readmission — everything gets worse with delirium.

**Delirium prevention and treatment:**

Like most things, an ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure; this is highly applicable here.

1. **SLEEP, SLEEP, SLEEP** – Nothing is more important to fixing a delirious brain than *good quality sleep.* Its absence is a primary cause, and rarely does one improve without it.
   - The hospital seems almost designed to interrupt sleep. Constant noise, light, staff members, testing, procedures, medical conditions, and medication side effects come together to ruin a hospitalized patient's sleep.
   - Our brains heal during sleep, specifically deep sleep (N3 — Slow Wave Sleep). Sleep cycles take 90-120 minutes to complete and repeat throughout the night until morning. N3 (deep sleep) occurs about 60-90 minutes after falling asleep. Light sleep (N1 & N2) can easily be interrupted; deep sleep is not so easily interrupted. In a hospital, frequent stimuli awaken people (they may not even realize it) and either pause or reset their sleep progression. This means that after just a few days in the hospital, having spent minimal time in deep sleep, your brain works as if you haven't slept in days. Imagine trying to learn a new task or process a flood of new information having not slept in days — now make your brain older, put it outside its familiar environment, under the influence of medications you may have never had before, dealing with new, possibly life-changing information.
   - Naps under 30 minutes are OK. Long naps should be avoided, as they disrupt the circadian sleep cycle, leading to people being awake more at night, which further worsens delirium.
   - See the section on Sleep for more details.
2. **Medications** – As outlined above, many of the medications we use have negative impacts on a patient's mental state. The more we use, the worse it becomes. Limiting medications as much as possible is necessary to improve delirium.
3. **Environmental changes** – Environmental changes both contribute to and help alleviate delirium. Positive changes include:
   - Reinforcing circadian rhythms — during the day, lights on, shades open (sunlight is important; going outside if able is great), out of bed in a chair, doing something mentally stimulating (reading, word search, crossword). Watching TV is OK, but because it's so passive, it does not maintain alertness.
   - Ensuring the patient has glasses and hearing aids (with working batteries) so they can process their environment.
   - Redirection/reorientation — as a patient starts to mentally stray, redirect them with correct information.
     - Don't argue with patients. If delirium has progressed to the point they are argumentative, a more convincing argument won't work. Redirect the conversation elsewhere or just dismiss it and move on.
4. **GET OUT OF THE HOSPITAL** – Not surprisingly, if the hospital is making the patient worse, the sooner they get out, the better. Waiting for delirium to resolve before going home is ill-advised. Staying longer to "fix" delirium usually makes it worse for the reasons cited above. It is a matter of physician judgment as to when the balance of medical stability and delirium warrants discharge.
   - About half of patients with hospital-acquired delirium will leave the hospital still delirious to some degree.
   - Home is best for the patient. They need a familiar environment and familiar faces.
   - Transitioning to rehab can *increase* delirium because of the change in environment. If discharge to rehab is medically advisable, having family at the rehab facility as much as possible for the first few days can significantly help with the transition.

**Things NOT to do:**

- Staying hospitalized longer than medically necessary.
- Using medications with potentially offending side effects when other options exist.
- Staying up late or ignoring the importance of sleep.
- Keeping hospital lighting at the same level 24 hours a day.
- Staying in bed all day watching TV.
- Not having glasses/hearing aids available.
- Arguing with the delirious patient.
- Not working with staff to mobilize out of bed.
- Thinking head imaging is helpful. Delirium is not a structural problem; it's not a stroke. Head CTs (and brain MRIs) are normal in delirium. Family members often ask for "brain scans," thinking it will "show something" and be helpful. If concerned, discuss it with the doctor.
- Restraints — when a patient is so combative they become a danger to themselves, they can be placed in restraints (fabric shackles at the wrist/ankle tying them to the bed). I hate them. They are undignified and often make things worse — imagine trying to wake from a dream tied to the bed; you'd freak out too. They are unfortunately sometimes necessary; however, as little use as possible is always the goal.
