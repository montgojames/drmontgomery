Sepsis is a feared topic for patients and providers alike. It is also one of the more misunderstood topics by patients. Sepsis is a systemic (full-body) response to an infection. For instance, if you have burning when you urinate, that would be a urinary tract infection. Add in a fever, chills, and a fast heart rate, and you now have sepsis from that UTI.

**Sepsis criteria:** An identified or suspected source of infection, AND 2 of the following:

1. Temperature > 100.4°F (38°C)
2. Heart rate > 90
3. Respiratory rate > 20
4. White blood count < 4 or > 12

There are other criteria used for sepsis, such as qSOFA and CURB-65. These were created in population studies to calculate mortality risk. Sepsis is important to recognize, and treatment should begin early. With time, the opportunity to intervene decreases and the risk of death increases.

Treatment involves antibiotics to cover the suspected source as soon as possible, and intravenous fluids to increase one's blood volume before blood pressure falls.

One of the major issues with sepsis, from a patient perspective, is that a single term encompasses a wide spectrum of illness. This is partially related to how money has become involved in healthcare. The government pays hospitals more money to take care of septic patients, as they require more resources — this makes sense. However, what it has led to is that every person who can squeeze into the criteria gets labeled "septic," which increases reimbursement. A patient can have a bloodstream staph infection at death's door, or be home with a cold and a low-grade fever and quick pulse — they are both "septic." This is why it takes a provider to recognize and treat the degree of illness. Everything being sepsis is analogous to having a fear of every kitten or house cat because your only understanding of cats is lions and tigers. The broad generalization removes nuance.
